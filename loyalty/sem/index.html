Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)

SEM distinguishes between what you can observe (manifest variables) and what you can’t directly measure (latent variables) like true loyalty or satisfaction.

! Why This Matters

A customer who appears “loyal” based on transaction volume alone might actually be transactionally loyal — driven by convenience or lack of competition — and may defect as soon as a competitor opens nearby.

  • Emotional Loyalty: Brand affinity, genuine preference, resistant to competitor offers
  • Transactional Loyalty: Convenience-driven, price-sensitive, high defection risk
  • SEM uses simultaneous equations to model how different factors cause loyalty types
  • Path analysis shows causal relationships that simple models miss

Causal Path Workbench — Draw the Model, Let the Data Speak

This works the way a real SEM analysis does. (1) Draw the causal DAG — variables and directed arrows. (2) Load data — upload any CSV or load the built-in dataset; the sample size N is read straight from the file (its number of rows) and column names are matched to your variables. (3) Press Fit Model — the engine computes the covariance matrix S and the correlation matrix from the data, estimates every path by maximum likelihood, and paints the standardized coefficients back onto your arrows: a 1 standard-deviation increase in the cause moves the effect by β standard deviations. You also get every variable’s total causal effect on your designated target and a live χ²/df/p test of whether your structure can reproduce the data.

3 · Fit the model

The fitted DAG — standardized path coefficients

The same structure and paths you drew, with the maximum-likelihood estimate printed on each arrow: a 1-standard-deviation increase in the cause changes the effect by β standard deviations, holding its other modeled causes fixed.

S matrix — covariance (computed from the data)

The actual sample covariance matrix of your modeled variables (divisor N − 1). This — together with N — is all the information the fit uses.

Correlation matrix (computed from the data)

S rescaled to unit variances. Amber italic cells are correlations between variables with no direct arrow in your model — if your DAG fits, these arise through chains and shared causes alone.

Causal effects

Goodness of fit — can your structure reproduce the data?

χ² = (N − 1) · FML with N read from the dataset, df = distinct moments − free parameters. Every missing arrow among downstream variables is a testable constraint; variables with no incoming arrow (exogenous) covary freely, as in standard path analysis.

Try it in 60 seconds: recover the hidden causal process

Press Load Default Dataset (400 customers), then click + on all four column chips to put them on the canvas. Draw satisfaction → loyalty, convenience → loyalty, loyalty → repurchase, and convenience → repurchase. Double-click repurchase and tick Is Target. Press Fit Model: the arrows light up with β ≈ 0.50, 0.35, 0.71, 0.18, and the test reads χ²(1) ≈ 1.3, p ≈ .26 — the structure is consistent with the data. Satisfaction’s total effect on repurchase (≈ 0.35) flows entirely through loyalty. Now delete convenience → repurchase and refit: χ²(2) ≈ 30, p < .001 — the data reject that one missing arrow.

Strategic Implication

Different loyalty types require different interventions. Transactionally loyal customers need barriers to switching; emotionally loyal customers need brand reinforcement — not discounts that might cheapen the relationship.
