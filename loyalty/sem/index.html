Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)

SEM distinguishes between what you can observe (manifest variables) and what you can’t directly measure (latent variables) like true loyalty or satisfaction.

! Why This Matters

A customer who appears “loyal” based on transaction volume alone might actually be transactionally loyal — driven by convenience or lack of competition — and may defect as soon as a competitor opens nearby.

  • Emotional Loyalty: Brand affinity, genuine preference, resistant to competitor offers
  • Transactional Loyalty: Convenience-driven, price-sensitive, high defection risk
  • SEM uses simultaneous equations to model how different factors cause loyalty types
  • Path analysis shows causal relationships that simple models miss

SEM / DAG Workbench — Entirely In The Browser

A self-contained path-model builder: no files, no uploads. (1) Create variables: click “+ Add Variable” and name your nodes (A, B, C… or anything you like). (2) Type your observed correlations into the grid that appears — that grid is the sample matrix S. (3) Draw the causal structure: click one node, then another, to add a directed path; cycles are rejected so the graph stays a recursive DAG and the drawn arrows form the structural matrix B. The engine then fits your DAG to your typed correlations by maximum likelihood — live, on every change — annotating each arrow with its estimated path coefficient and reporting χ², df, and the p-value of your structure against your data.

1 · Variables & Causal Structure (DAG → B matrix)

Double-click a node to rename or delete it. Single-click one node, then another to draw a directed path; click a path’s arrow (or its chip below) to delete it; drawing an existing path in reverse flips it; cycles are rejected. Drag nodes to arrange your diagram. Variables with no incoming path are exogenous and freely correlated (dashed arcs).

2 · Observed Correlations — the S matrix

Type the correlations you observed between your variables (each between −1 and 1; the diagonal is fixed at 1). This grid is the sample covariance matrix S the model is tested against. The χ² test also needs the sample size those correlations came from.

Add at least two variables to start.

3 · Live Fit — Goodness of Fit (Maximum-Likelihood χ²)

Model Comparison (Δχ² Test)

Save the current model as Model A, then change the structure — the live fit is always Model B. If one model is a simplified version of the other (same variables, a subset of its paths, no variable switching exogenous/endogenous status), the nested difference test applies: Δχ² = χ²restricted − χ²general, Δdf = dfrestricted − dfgeneral. Non-nested pairs are compared with AIC/BIC. Saved models are automatically re-estimated when you edit the grid, so both χ² values always refer to the same S.

Parameter Estimates

Try it in 30 seconds

Create three variables A, B, C. In the grid, set A–B = 0.60, B–C = 0.50, A–C = 0.30. Draw the chain A → B → C. A chain implies corr(A,C) = 0.60 × 0.50 = 0.30 — exactly what you typed — so χ²(1) ≈ 0 and p ≈ 1: your DAG is consistent with your data. Now change A–C to 0.00: the chain over-predicts that correlation and χ² jumps — the test rejects the structure. Add a direct path A → C and fit is perfect again, but saturated (df = 0): with as many parameters as data points, fit can no longer be tested.

Strategic Implication

Different loyalty types require different interventions. Transactionally loyal customers need barriers to switching; emotionally loyal customers need brand reinforcement — not discounts that might cheapen the relationship.
