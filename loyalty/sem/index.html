Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)

SEM distinguishes between what you can observe (manifest variables) and what you can’t directly measure (latent variables) like true loyalty or satisfaction.

! Why This Matters

A customer who appears “loyal” based on transaction volume alone might actually be transactionally loyal — driven by convenience or lack of competition — and may defect as soon as a competitor opens nearby.

  • Emotional Loyalty: Brand affinity, genuine preference, resistant to competitor offers
  • Transactional Loyalty: Convenience-driven, price-sensitive, high defection risk
  • SEM uses simultaneous equations to model how different factors cause loyalty types
  • Path analysis shows causal relationships that simple models miss

SEM Workbench — Draw Your Own Model

A synthetic loyalty survey is loaded below: four latent constructs, each measured by three survey items. Draw the structural model you believe in — click one construct, then another, to add a causal path — then fit it to the data by maximum likelihood and judge it with standard goodness-of-fit statistics. Constructs with no incoming path are treated as exogenous and freely correlated (dashed arcs), as in Stata or lavaan. Competing models you fit are collected in the comparison table so you can argue about which structure the data actually supports.

Click a construct to select it (it glows), then click another to draw a path between them. Click a path’s arrow — or its chip below — to delete it. Drawing a path that already exists in reverse flips it. Cycles are rejected: models must be recursive (a DAG). The three small squares under each construct are its survey items; their loadings are estimated with the model.

Spoiler: the true data-generating model

The survey is simulated from: Satisfaction → Emotional Loyalty (0.60), Emotional Loyalty → Behavioral Loyalty (0.50), Convenience → Behavioral Loyalty (0.35), plus a small direct Satisfaction → Behavioral Loyalty (0.15) path, with corr(Satisfaction, Convenience) = 0.30. All latents have unit variance; loadings range 0.70–0.85. Models that omit the small direct path still fit acceptably — a good reminder that fit indices reward approximation, not truth. Models that omit a major path (or point it the wrong way around a mediator) get punished.

Strategic Implication

Different loyalty types require different interventions. Transactionally loyal customers need barriers to switching; emotionally loyal customers need brand reinforcement — not discounts that might cheapen the relationship.
